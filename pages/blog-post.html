The 7 Design Principles That Separate Good from Great

Alexandra Chen
CEO & Design Lead at Signflow
Share:

Most websites look decent. A few look extraordinary. After analyzing over 500 high-converting websites — and designing dozens ourselves — we've identified the exact principles that separate them. Some are obvious. Some will surprise you.

Design isn't decoration. It's decision-making made visible. Every pixel, every spacing choice, every color combination communicates something to your visitor, whether you intended it to or not.

"Design is not just what it looks like and feels like. Design is how it works." — Steve Jobs

Here are the seven principles we've found matter most — and how to apply them immediately, regardless of your skill level.

The 7 Principles

01

Hierarchy tells the story

Your most important message should be visually dominant. If everything screams for attention, nothing gets it. Use size, weight, and contrast to create a clear visual path from most important to least. Test it: squint at your design. What do you see first? That should be your primary message.

02

Whitespace is not wasted space

Amateur designers fill space. Expert designers leave it deliberately. Whitespace creates breathing room, groups related elements, and directs attention. The most premium-feeling brands use generous whitespace — it signals confidence and quality.

03

Consistency builds trust

Every inconsistency — a different button style, an unexpected font, an off-brand color — erodes trust. Your design system should be a strict vocabulary. Use a limited palette, defined type scale, and consistent spacing. Constraints are features, not limitations.

04

Color communicates before content does

Visitors form a visual impression in under 50 milliseconds — before they read a single word. Your color palette sets the emotional tone. Blue signals trust. Green signals growth. Dark themes signal premium or technical. Choose intentionally and limit yourself to 2–3 core colors.

05

Typography is 90% of design

A well-chosen typeface with good sizing, line-height, and letter-spacing will outperform a mediocre design with beautiful imagery every time. Invest in typography first. Use a display font for headlines and a humanist sans for body text. Never set body text above 18px or below 15px.

06

Motion adds meaning, not decoration

Animations should communicate — revealing new content, confirming actions, providing feedback. If removing an animation doesn't change the user's understanding, it probably shouldn't be there. One excellent, purposeful transition is worth more than ten decorative ones.

07

Design for the worst case first

What does your design look like on a slow 3G connection? With accessibility settings on? On a tiny phone? Great design works everywhere — it's a minimum bar, not a bonus. Test on real devices, at real speeds, with real users before you call anything done.

💡

Quick win: Run your website through Google PageSpeed Insights, Lighthouse, and Wave (accessibility) right now. Fix the top 3 issues from each. The improvements to your design and performance will be immediate and significant.

Putting it together

These principles don't operate in isolation — they compound. A site with excellent hierarchy AND generous whitespace AND consistent typography communicates premium quality at every touchpoint. The gap between "looks decent" and "looks extraordinary" is usually not one big decision but twenty small ones, each made with intention.

Pick one principle this week. Apply it obsessively. Then move to the next. In seven weeks, your design will be unrecognizable — in the best possible way.
